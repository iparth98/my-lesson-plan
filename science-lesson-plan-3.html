Applications of Sound (Science, Mathematics)

  Big Ideas:

  • Light and sounds are forms of energy with specific properties
  • Technological innovations involving light and sound have an impact on the environment
 Learning Objectives:
The students will be able to:
  • Describe properties of sound
  • Explain how sound travels through waves and is transferred into different types of matter
  • Describe the pattern in which sound waves travel
Specific Expectations:
  • Describe properties of sound, including that sound travels through a medium as a wave and that a sound can be absorbed, reflected and modified
  • Describe how different objects and materials interact with light and sound energy
Cross Curricular Connections:

The STEM infusion will be Math as students will be analyzing the movement of waves by looking at the amplitude and frequency of the waves.

  Instructional Strategies:
Hand-on activities-creating model, classroom discussion, Think Pair Share, small group presentation and giving each other peer feedback

Hands –On-Activity- String Telephones/ Shoe Box Guitar

This group will read the below instructions to create a Shoe Box Guitar.

Materials: Sturdy shoebox, 8-10 rubber bands of varying widths, pencil

Instruction:

Place rubber bands of different widths around the shoebox, with thicker ones at one end and thinner ones at the other end. Place a pencil underneath all the rubber bands on the end near the shoebox hole, similar to the bridge on a guitar.

How to Play Your Instrument: Pluck the rubber bands to play.

Explanation:

Lead a class discussion on the practical uses of light and sound technology. Introduce concepts such as Morse code, binary signals, and the scientific principles behind musical instruments.

The Morse Code is a language that people use to communicate with each other. through telegraph, radio, satellite, or with light. The language is a series of. short and long bursts that are heard or seen. The short bursts of sound or light are called “dit”, and the long bursts are called “dah”.

Extension:

Challenge students to design and build their own invention using light and sound, and present it to the class.

Differentiation

Universal Design for Learning (UDL): Provide step-by-step instructions with visual diagrams for hands-on activities. Offer alternative materials or tools for students with physical disabilities.

English Language Learners (ELL): Use real-life examples and demonstrations to illustrate abstract concepts. Provide bilingual glossaries or vocabulary cards related to technological terms.

Special Education: Offer simplified versions of hands-on activities with additional support from teaching assistants. Allow extra time for students to complete tasks at their own pace.

Assessments
Formative:

Monitor student progress during hands-on activities and discussions. Use anecdotal notes to track understanding of practical applications of light and sound.

Summative:

Evaluate student inventions based on creativity, functionality, and understanding of scientific principles. Use a rubric to assess presentation skills and the ability to explain how their invention works.

Conclusion

This lesson plan engages students in hands-on activities to explore practical uses of light and sound, fostering understanding and creativity. Through activities like making string telephones and discussing Morse code, students deepen their comprehension while developing problem-solving skills. With inclusive instruction and assessments, all students participate and showcase their understanding by designing and presenting their inventions.
